### 1. The "Alaska Solar Math" (Flat vs. Pole)

In a place like Fairbanks during December, the sun only hugs the horizon at an angle of about **2.6° to 5°**.

- **Flat Mounted (The "Drive" Mode):** While driving or in the shoulder seasons (March/September), flat is fine. But in the dead of winter, flat panels will produce **almost zero power**. They are effectively just expensive roof armor at that point.
- **The Pole/Disconnect Strategy:** This is your "saving grace." By moving them to poles and tilting them to **~65-75°**, you can capture the low-angle sun. Since you’re building for two adults and two dogs, you’ll likely be using ~6-8 kWh per day. A 4000W array tilted correctly can actually keep up with that, even with only 3.5 hours of daylight.

### 2. The 48V Battery Bank (The "2-Day" Reserve)

To run "main stuff" (lights, fridge, starlink, water pump, diesel heater) for 2 days without sun, you need a specific capacity.

- **The Target:** Roughly **15-20 kWh** of storage.
- **The 48V Configuration:** At 48V, a **300Ah to 400Ah** Lithium (LiFePO4) bank will give you about **15.3 kWh to 20.4 kWh**.
- **The Weight Factor:** This bank will weigh roughly **350-450 lbs**. Since you're boxing the frame, you have the perfect structural "pockets" to mount these between the rails, low to the ground, to keep your center of gravity stable.

### 3. Critical Winter Survival Logic

- **Self-Heating Batteries:** In Alaska, **never** buy standard Lithium batteries. If they drop below 32°F (0°C), they cannot take a charge and the BMS will shut them down. You need **self-heating** models or to build your battery box inside the "thermal envelope" of your insulated cabin.
- **The "Ground Loop" Problem:** When you move panels to poles, you’re creating a long run of wire. At 48V (or higher string voltage), you'll minimize line loss, but make sure your disconnects are **weather-sealed Anderson connectors** or similar heavy-duty plugs that won't seize up in the ice.

### 4. Integration with the Build

Since you mentioned the 48V system:

- **Alternator Charging:** Your 6.0L (or future DT360/466) has a 12V charging system. You'll need a **12V-to-48V DC-DC charger** (like a Victron Orion) so you can charge that massive battery bank while you're driving up the Alcan.
- **Redundancy:** With 4000W of solar, you are in the "over-paneled" category. This is good because even on a cloudy Alaskan day, you might still pull 400W—enough to keep the "vampire loads" from draining your 2-day reserve.
