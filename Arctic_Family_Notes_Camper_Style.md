## The "Skeleton" Concept: Multi-Material Fusion

Since we aren't using a mold, we are building a **semi-monocoque frame**. The aluminum cage provides the structural rigidity, while the skin and interior wood provide the shear strength (preventing the "box" from racking).

### 1. The Aluminum Rib-Cage (The Frame)

- **Vertical Members:** Use 1" x 2" rectangular aluminum tubing (6061-T6) on 16-inch centers.
- **Corner Radius:** Instead of a sharp 90° box (which catches wind and looks "DIY"), we should design **faceted corners** (three 30° angles) to mimic that sleek Revcon nose and roofline.
- **Thermal Break:** Crucial for Alaska. You *must* place a high-density foam tape or rubber strip between the aluminum studs and the interior wood. If you don't, the aluminum will act as a "thermal bridge," and you'll have frost lines on your interior walls in the winter.

### 2. The Exterior Skin (The Armor)

- **Material:** .040 or .050 gauge aluminum sheets.
- **Attachment:** Do not just screw them on. Use a combination of **3M VHB Tape** (Very High Bond) and structural rivets. The VHB tape allows the skin to expand and contract with the temperature swings in Alaska without buckling the metal.
- **Seams:** Overlap the sheets like shingles (top over bottom) and use a high-grade polyurethane sealant (like SikaFlex 221).

### 3. The Interior "Shear" Wall

- **Material:** 1/4" or 3/8" Baltic Birch plywood.
- **Purpose:** This isn't just for looks. Once you glue and screw this to the aluminum frame, the entire structure becomes incredibly rigid. It stops the "sway" that long-wheelbase 6x6s often experience.

## Phase 1: The 6x6 Chassis Physics

The biggest challenge with a 6x6 on an F-350 frame isn't just the length; it's **Frame Flex vs. Box Rigidity**.

- **The "Three-Point" Mount:** Since you're a welder, you know that truck frames are designed to twist. If you bolt a rigid aluminum box directly to the frame, the first time you cross a ditch, the box will crack. You’ll want to design a **sub-frame** that mounts to the F-350 frame using a pivot or spring-mount system at the front, allowing the truck to twist underneath the "house."
- **The Tandem Setup:** For a 6x6, a **Walking Beam** or a **Trailing Arm Air Suspension** is your best bet for Alaska. It keeps all four rear tires on the ground for maximum traction in snow/mud and lets you level the camper perfectly when you park for the night.

## Phase 2: The Aluminum "Exoskeleton" Blueprint

To get that Revcon look without a mold, we’ll use a **Faceted Radius** design. Instead of a sharp 90-degree edge, we use 45-degree transitions.

### The Framing Logic

1. **Lower Perimeter (The Sill):** 2" x 4" x 3/16" Aluminum Rectangular Tubing. This is your "foundation" that bolts to your sub-frame.
2. **Wall Studs:** 1" x 1.5" x 1/8" Wall Tubing. Spaced at 16" on center. Double up studs around window and door cutouts.
3. **The "Roll Cage" Roof:** Arched 1" x 1" square tubing. An arch is structurally superior for snow load (crucial for Alaska) and naturally sheds water.

### The Skinning Process (Master Carpenter + Welder Synergy)

- **Adhesive over Fasteners:** Use **3M VHB tape** on the studs before laying the .050 aluminum skin. This prevents "oil canning" (the popping sound metal makes when it expands in the sun) and creates a waterproof seal without drilling 5,000 holes.
- **The Rivet "Stitch":** Use structural buck rivets or Olympic rivets only at the corners and seams for that aircraft/Revcon aesthetic.
